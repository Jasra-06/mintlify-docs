Your internship is as much about learning as it is about contributing. You'll grow fastest when you combine hands-on project work with intentional, structured study. This page points you to the best resources for technical skills, soft skills, and domain knowledge — and explains how to make the most of the dedicated learning time available to you.

<Tip>
  Set a specific learning goal at the start of each week. For example: "Complete one module on REST API design" or "Read two chapters of the recommended book." Small, consistent goals compound into significant skill gains over the course of your internship.
</Tip>

## Internal knowledge base

Your team's internal wiki (Confluence or Notion) is the first place to look for any topic related to your team's systems, processes, or decisions. It typically includes:

- Architecture diagrams and design documents
- How-to guides for internal tooling and deployments
- Meeting notes and decision logs
- Onboarding materials written by previous interns

Ask your mentor for the link to your team's space and spend time in your first week reading through the most relevant sections. Understanding how and why decisions were made will help you contribute meaningful work faster.

## Learning paths by skill area

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Technical skills">
    Whether you're deepening existing knowledge or learning something new for your project, these free and low-cost platforms cover the technical fundamentals you'll need.

    **Online courses**

    - [freeCodeCamp](https://www.freecodecamp.org) — Free, project-based courses on web development, Python, data analysis, and more. Certificates are respected by employers.
    - [The Odin Project](https://www.theodinproject.com) — A structured, open-source curriculum for full-stack web development.
    - [Coursera](https://www.coursera.org) — University-backed courses on computer science, machine learning, and data engineering. Many courses are free to audit.
    - [edX](https://www.edx.org) — Similar to Coursera, with a strong catalogue of CS and engineering courses from MIT, Harvard, and others.
    - [MIT OpenCourseWare](https://ocw.mit.edu) — Free lecture notes, problem sets, and videos from actual MIT courses.

    **Reference and documentation**

    - [MDN Web Docs](https://developer.mozilla.org) — The definitive reference for HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.
    - [DevDocs](https://devdocs.io) — A single searchable interface for documentation across hundreds of languages and frameworks.
    - Official docs for every language or framework you use during your project. Your mentor can point you to the most important ones.

    **Communities and forums**

    - [Stack Overflow](https://stackoverflow.com) — Search before you post; most common questions are already answered.
    - [GitHub Discussions](https://github.com) — Many open-source projects host community Q&A directly on GitHub.
    - [Dev.to](https://dev.to) — Practical articles and tutorials written by practising engineers.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Soft skills">
    Technical ability gets you hired; soft skills determine how far you go. Use your internship to practise communication, collaboration, and professional habits in a real work environment.

    **Online courses**

    - [LinkedIn Learning](https://www.linkedin.com/learning) — Strong library of courses on communication, time management, presenting, and working in teams. Many universities and companies provide free access — ask your program coordinator.
    - [Coursera — Learning How to Learn](https://www.coursera.org/learn/learning-how-to-learn) — A highly rated free course that teaches evidence-based study and retention strategies.

    **Books**

    - *The Effective Engineer* by Edmond Lau — Practical advice on high-leverage work habits for software practitioners.
    - *Atomic Habits* by James Clear — A framework for building consistent, compounding habits that applies directly to your learning goals.
    - *Dare to Lead* by Brené Brown — Useful for understanding how to give and receive feedback and how to communicate under pressure.

    **Practice opportunities during your internship**

    - Volunteer to present your work at a team meeting or demo.
    - Write a short update email to your mentor summarising your weekly progress (see [Communication](/resources/communication) for templates).
    - Ask for feedback after every significant deliverable — and act on it visibly.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Domain knowledge">
    Understanding the industry and business context behind your team's work makes you a more effective contributor. Prioritise the resources your mentor recommends, then supplement with broader reading.

    **Getting context fast**

    - Read your team's internal documentation from the past six months before diving into external resources. Knowing the team's specific problems is more valuable than generic industry knowledge.
    - Ask your mentor to recommend two or three documents, blog posts, or papers that explain why the team works the way it does.
    - Review any public-facing product or service you're helping to build. Use it as a real customer would.

    **Staying current**

    - Follow relevant newsletters for your domain (e.g., *TLDR* for software, *The Batch* by deeplearning.ai for ML, *Stratechery* for product strategy).
    - Set a 15-minute slot each week to read one article or blog post in your domain and take a brief note on what you learned.
    - Browse your company's engineering blog if one exists — it's a direct window into the challenges your team has already solved.

    **Books and long-form reading**

    - Ask your mentor for one book recommendation relevant to your team's domain. Many engineers have a go-to title they give to new team members.
    - If your team works on distributed systems, *Designing Data-Intensive Applications* by Martin Kleppmann is widely considered essential reading.
    - For product-focused roles, *Inspired* by Marty Cagan gives a strong grounding in how successful product teams operate.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Using your dedicated learning time

Most internship programs allocate time each week for self-directed learning — typically two to four hours. Use this time deliberately:

- Block the time in your calendar and treat it like a meeting you can't skip.
- Focus on skills that directly apply to your current project or upcoming tasks.
- Keep a short learning log (a doc or notebook entry) noting what you studied and your key takeaways. Share it with your mentor during your 1-on-1.

## Asking your mentor for learning recommendations

Your mentor is one of your best learning resources. They know exactly which skills matter most in your team's context and can point you to the highest-value material. When asking for recommendations:

- Be specific about the skill gap you're trying to close, not just the general topic.
- Tell them how much time you have each week for self-study so they can calibrate the scope.
- Follow up after completing something they recommended — it shows engagement and opens the door to deeper conversations.

A simple way to bring it up during your 1-on-1: *"I'm trying to get better at [X]. Do you have a course or resource you'd recommend, given the way our team works?"*
