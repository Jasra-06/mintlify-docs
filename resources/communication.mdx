Clear, proactive communication is one of the most important skills you'll demonstrate during your internship. Your team can't support you if they don't know what you're working on or where you're stuck. This page explains which channels to use, how to write messages that get quick responses, and how to stay visible and connected throughout your time here.

<Note>
  This team operates async-first. That means most communication happens through written messages rather than real-time conversations, and people are not expected to respond immediately. Respect your colleagues' focus time by defaulting to async channels unless the situation is genuinely urgent.
</Note>

## Communication channels at a glance

You'll use three main types of communication tools during your internship:

- **Slack or Microsoft Teams** — Day-to-day async messaging. Use this for questions, updates, and quick coordination with your team.
- **Email** — Formal and cross-team communication. Use this for meeting requests, official updates, and anything that needs a written record.
- **Zoom or Google Meet** — Live meetings. Use this for standups, 1-on-1s with your mentor, demos, and anything that benefits from real-time discussion.

## When to use which channel

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Slack or Teams">
    Use Slack or Teams for:

    - Quick questions that need context from a teammate
    - Sharing links, files, or short updates with the team
    - Asking for a code review or a second pair of eyes
    - Informal check-ins or non-urgent blockers
    - Participating in team and intern channels

    **Tips for effective Slack messages:**

    - Use threads to keep conversations organised. Reply in a thread rather than sending a new top-level message.
    - Use `@mentions` sparingly — only tag someone if the message genuinely requires their attention.
    - Write one complete message rather than several short follow-ups. Include enough context so the recipient doesn't need to ask follow-up questions.
    - Add a reaction (✅, 👀) to confirm you've seen a message when a response isn't required.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Email">
    Use email for:

    - Meeting requests or calendar invites
    - Official updates to your mentor or program coordinator
    - Any communication that needs a formal written record
    - Reaching people outside your immediate team

    **Tips for effective emails:**

    - Write clear subject lines that summarise the action required: "Meeting request: project kickoff — [your name]" or "Status update: week 3 progress".
    - Keep the body concise. State your purpose in the first sentence, provide the necessary context, and end with a clear next step or question.
    - Use bullet points for lists of items — they're easier to scan than dense paragraphs.
    - Reply to the same email thread when following up. Don't start a new thread for the same topic.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Zoom or Google Meet">
    Use video calls for:

    - Weekly 1-on-1s with your mentor
    - Team standups and sprint ceremonies
    - Project demos and presentations
    - Discussions that would take longer to resolve over text

    **Meeting etiquette:**

    - Join one to two minutes early so the meeting starts on time.
    - Keep your camera on by default — it signals engagement and helps build rapport with your team.
    - Mute yourself when you're not speaking to reduce background noise.
    - Take notes during the meeting, especially action items. Send a short follow-up message summarising decisions and next steps if you were leading the meeting.
    - If you need to leave early, let the organiser know at the start of the call.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Giving status updates

Proactive status updates keep your mentor and team informed without requiring them to chase you. Aim to give a written update at least once a week. A good update covers three things:

1. **What you completed** since the last update
2. **What you're working on** now
3. **Any blockers** or decisions you need help with

Keep updates brief and factual. You don't need to justify every hour — just give enough information for your mentor to know if you're on track and whether you need support.

### Weekly status update template

```text
Subject: Week [N] update — [Your Name]

Hi [Mentor's name],

Here's my update for the week:

Completed:
- [Task or milestone you finished]
- [Another completed item]

In progress:
- [What you're currently working on and expected completion]

Blockers:
- [Any issue preventing progress — or "None this week"]

Questions:
- [Any decisions or clarifications you need]

[Your name]
```

## Email templates for common situations

### Requesting a meeting

```text
Subject: Meeting request: [topic] — [Your Name]

Hi [Name],

I'd like to schedule a [15/30]-minute call to discuss [brief topic description].

I'm available [day] between [time range] or [alternative]. Let me know what works for you, or feel free to send a calendar invite.

Thanks,
[Your name]
```

### Asking for feedback on your work

```text
Subject: Feedback request: [project or deliverable name]

Hi [Name],

I've completed [brief description of what you finished] and would appreciate your feedback before I move forward.

You can find it here: [link or attachment]

Specifically, I'd find it helpful to know:
- [Specific question 1]
- [Specific question 2]

No rush — whenever you have 10 minutes this week works well.

Thanks,
[Your name]
```

### Following up on an unanswered message

```text
Subject: Following up: [original subject line]

Hi [Name],

I wanted to follow up on my message from [date] about [topic]. I know you're busy — I just want to make sure it didn't get buried.

[Restate your question or request in one sentence.]

Let me know if you need any additional context. Thanks.

[Your name]
```

## Response time expectations and escalation

<Warning>
  Most team members respond to Slack messages within a few hours during working hours, and to emails within one to two business days. If you need a response faster than that — for example, because you're completely blocked and can't make progress — say so explicitly in your message: "I'm blocked on this until I hear back, so any time you have today would help." If you still don't hear back after 48 hours, it's appropriate to escalate to your program coordinator.
</Warning>

If you're blocked and waiting on a response, don't sit idle. Use the time to make progress on another task, review documentation, or identify alternative approaches. Mention the blocker in your next status update so your mentor has visibility.

## Writing messages that get responses

The most common reason messages go unanswered is that they're hard to act on. Apply these principles to every message you send:

- **Front-load the key information.** Put your question or request in the first sentence, not at the end.
- **Be specific.** "Can you help me with the API?" is harder to act on than "I'm getting a 401 error when calling the `/users` endpoint with the token I generated yesterday — can you point me to the right auth flow?"
- **Limit each message to one topic.** If you have multiple questions, decide which one is most urgent and ask that one first.
- **State what you've already tried.** This shows initiative and helps the person give a more useful answer.
